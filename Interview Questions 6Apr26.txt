Agile Methodology


Agile Methodology is a way to manage projects by breaking them into smaller parts. 
It focuses on working together and making constant improvements. Teams plan, work on the project, and then review in a repeating cycle.

It focuses on delivering smaller pieces of work regularly instead of one big launch.
This allows teams to adapt to changes quickly and provide customer value faster.
Major companies like Facebook, Google, and Amazon use Agile because of its adaptability and customer-focused approach.
Prioritize flexibility, collaboration, and customer satisfaction.
In the traditional waterfall model, we first get all client requirements and do not allow changes during the project

Agile Development Stages
1. Requirement Gathering
2. Design
3. Development (Coding)
4. Testing
5. Deployment
6. Review (Maintenance)

Types of Agile Methodologies
Kanban
Scrum

Applications of the Agile Methodology?

Unclear or Changing Requirements: Agile is great for projects with requirements that aren't well-defined or might change.
Complex Projects: It's good for big, complex projects by breaking them into smaller pieces.
Customer Focus: Use Agile when making customers happy is a priority and you want to involve them throughout.
Quick Time-to-Market: If you need to get your product out fast, Agile can help.
Small to Medium Teams: Agile works well for teams of a few to a few dozen people.
Collaboration: Agile promotes working together and open communication.
Regular Updates: If you want to check progress often and make changes as needed.
Continuous Improvement: Agile fosters a culture of always getting better over time.

Declarative actions

Declarative Actions are an upgrade to UI Actions.

Declarative actions add custom functionality to your Workspace forms, fields, lists, and related lists without writing custom scripts or learning APIs. Declarative actions include: form and list actions, related list actions, and field decorators.

But things are changing. There are many new Next Experience-based UIs like Workspaces, Playbooks, and Catalog Wizards. Plus, users can build their own custom Next Experience-based UIs. UI Actions don’t work well with these new types of UIs because they’re so heavily based around the concept of lists and forms.


That’s where Declarative Actions come in. They’re more flexible and work with any style of UI, whether it be a list, or a form, or a playbook, or a catalog wizard. They are the future of performing actions in ServiceNow.

There are three different ways a DA can be implemented in Next Experience-based interfaces:

Client Script – to execute a UI Action-style script on the client-side.
Server Script – to execute a UI Action-style script on the server-side.
UXF Client Action – to dispatch an action in a Next Experience-based UI for handling by an event handler in UI Builder.

Disadvantages of Flow designer

Disadvantages of Flow Designer

There will always be a delay between the actual trigger and the start of the flow. 

Flow Designer is excellent for low-code automation but has limitations in performance, debugging, and handling complex logic compared to scripting approaches like Business Rules or Script Includes.


1. Performance Overhead
2. Limited Debugging Capabilities
3. Less Flexibility Compared to Scripting
4. Version Control Challenges
5. Governance & Sprawl Issues
6. Execution Context Limitations


1. Performance Overhead
Flows can be slower than Business Rules or Scripts
Each action runs as a separate transaction in some cases
Not ideal for high-volume or real-time processing

👉 Example: Bulk record updates are faster with Background Scripts or Fix Scripts.

🔻 2. Limited Debugging Capabilities
Debugging is not as powerful as scripting tools
Hard to trace complex logic across multiple subflows/actions
Execution details are sometimes not granular enough

🔻 3. Less Flexibility Compared to Scripting
Complex logic (loops, conditions, transformations) can become messy
Advanced developers often prefer:
Script Includes
Business Rules

👉 Flow Designer is low-code, but not always pro-code friendly

🔻 4. Version Control Challenges
Limited native support for:
Branching
Merging
Difficult to manage in team environments compared to code-based development

🔻 5. Governance & Sprawl Issues
Easy to create flows → leads to:
Too many flows
Duplicate logic
Poor maintainability

👉 Without governance, system becomes hard to manage

🔻 6. Execution Context Limitations
Runs in a specific context (system/user)
Sometimes lacks flexibility in:
impersonation
fine-grained control
